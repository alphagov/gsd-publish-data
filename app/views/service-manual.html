Publish data Publishing to Government Service Data

Published by:
Government Service Data team
Last updated:
31 January 2017

Give feedback about this page

You only need to publish to Government Service Data if your service is transactional.

A transactional service is one that lets a user update their status with government. This is distinct from a ‘look-up’ service, which provides users with guidance but doesn’t allow them to change their status.

Here are some examples of transactional services:

  • Making a claim
  • Applying for a licence
  • Buying a product
  • Booking a service
  • Applying for a benefit
  • Paying a fine or a tax bill

A user using this type of service successfully results in a transaction.

Types of information to be published

If your service is transactional, you must publish three types of information to Government Service Data:

  • information about the service
  • the KPIs
  • information about your data

Information about the service

Includes information on:

  • Your service’s purpose - why it was built, its policy objectives and the user need it meets
  • How it works - the processes and back-office operations that allow it to function
  • Information about the key user groups, such as where they live and the demographic they belong to
  • How frequently the service is used
  • How long, on average, it takes transactions handled by your service to end in an outcome

The KPIs

1. Number of transactions received, split by channel

This KPI records the number of transactions your service received, split by the following channels:

  • Online — do not include transactions where an online tool was used to fill out a paper form, or transactions received via web chat
  • Phone — do not include interactive voice response (IVR) or purely informational calls that don’t affect the outcome of a transaction
  • Paper form — include any sending method (post, email, fax, uploading)
  • Face-to-face — do not include transactions where the user handed in a paper form in person
  • Other

All numbers (total number of transactions received and number of transactions received through each channel) are part of the KPIs and are published.

2. Number of transactions ending in an outcome

The number of transactions that ended with the user’s status with government being updated.

For example, the user applies for a benefit and is either accepted or rejected. Even if the application is rejected, this is still a change and so a transaction has taken place.

3. Number of transactions ending in the user’s intended outcome

The number of transactions that ended with the user reaching the outcome they set out to achieve. For example, the user applies for a benefit and is accepted.

For metrics 2 and 3, data should relate to the month in which the transaction ended.

4. Number of phone calls received, split by reason for calling

The total number of calls your service received, split by the following reasons:

  • User called to get information - to check eligibility, for example, or ask for information they couldn’t get online
  • User called to chase progress
  • User called to challenge a decision
  • Other - for example, the user wanted to make a transaction via the phone, or needed to get help using the service

If you’re providing data based on a sample of calls, you also need to tell us your sample size. The KPI is the sample, not the overall figure.

Information about your data

This information is published alongside your data and allows other civil servants to understand its reliability and validity.

Include information on:

  • the organisation that owns the service (usually the department and/or agency that your service sits within)
  • how the data was collected (the methods, tools, sources and calculation methods used)

When must I publish it?

You need to publish a month’s worth of data on a monthly basis, two months in arrears. For example, data for February would be published in April.
